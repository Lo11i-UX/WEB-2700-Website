Part 2: Summary of Plain Language

Key Principles

  1. Know Your Audience
    • Purpose: Tailor your message to your readers’ needs, expectations, and background.
    • Benefit: Your audience wants to grasp the message quickly.
  2. Organize Your Text
    • Structure: Start with the most important points.
    • Tools:
      • Use headings and subheadings
      • Use bullet points and numbered lists
    • Result: Reduces the reader’s cognitive load.
  3. Use Plain and Simple Language
    • Avoid: Technical jargon, complex words, and convoluted sentences.
    • Goal: Keep your message clear and accessible.
  4. Use Active Voice
    • Advantage: Active voice makes your writing direct and energetic.
    • Note: While passive voice may appear occasionally, active voice is preferred.
  5. Stay Consistent
    • Why: Consistency in style and terminology enhances clarity.
    • Tip: Use uniform language throughout your document

Principle Table

Principle Benefit Tip
Know Your Audience Tailors content to reader needs Understand your audience
Organize Your Text Improves readability Use clear headings and lists
Use Simple Language Enhances clarity Avoid jargon and complexity
Use Active Voice Makes writing direct Prefer active constructions
Stay Consistent Improves coherence Maintain a uniform style

Conclusion

Plain language guidelines are essential tools for clear communication. They help you produce transparent and effective content. Although sticking to these principles is ideal, slight deviations may occur as you balance clarity with the nuances of language.

Part 3: Verbs Demystified

Active vs. Passive Voice

What is Active Voice?

  • Definition: In an active sentence, the subject performs the action.
  • Example: "The cat chased the mouse." (Here, "the cat" is doing the chasing.)

What is Passive Voice?

  • Definition: Passive sentences often use forms of "to be" (am, is, are, was, were) plus a past participle.
  • Example: "The mouse was chased by the cat." (Here, "the mouse" receives the action.)

How to Switch from Passive to Active:

  1. Step 1: Identify the subject (the doer).
    2. Passive: "The mouse was chased by the cat."
  2. Move the doer to the beginning of the sentence.
    3. Active: "The cat chased the mouse."
  3. Tip: If the doer is missing, add it.

Why Use Active Voice?

  • Benefits:
    • Clearer and more direct sentences
    • More engaging and powerful writing
    • Better for instructions, persuasion, and clear communication

Understanding Verb Tenses and Forms

Simple Verb Forms (Preferred for Clarity)

  • Simple Present:
    • Example: "walk"
    • Usage: Habits and general truths (e.g., "She walks to school every day.")
  • Simple Past:
    • Example: "walked"
    • Usage: Actions that happened in the past (e.g., "She walked to school yesterday.")
  • Simple Future:
    • Example: "will walk"
    • Usage: Actions that will occur (e.g., "She will walk to school tomorrow.")

Continuous Verb Forms

  • Definition: These forms show ongoing action.
  • Examples:
    • Present continuous: "is walking"
    • Past continuous: "was walking"
    • Future continuous: "will be walking"
  • Note: They can add complexity without adding clarity.

Perfect Verb Forms

  • Definition: These forms relate actions to another time.
  • Examples:
    • Present perfect: "has walked"
    • Past perfect: "had walked"
    • Future perfect: "will have walked"
  • Note: They often make sentences harder to understand when simple forms would do.

Perfect Continuous Forms

  • Definition: These combine perfect and continuous aspects (e.g., "has been walking").
  • Note: They often make sentences harder to understand when simple forms would do.

Recommendation for Clear Writing:

  • Use Simple Forms: They keep sentences direct and easy to read.
  • Reserve Complex Forms: Use them only when you need to express detailed time frames or continuous actions.
